Introduction

The local government architecture model (LGAM) is designed to be a guide to the public technology layers that are used to deliver local public services.

The LGAM is part of a wider vision for local government technology. It guides:

  • the Government Digital Service (GDS) in building common products, including those for local government
  • government departments that build products and services for councils
  • councils who want to work with other local authorities on products and services
  • vendors who need to make common capabilities, patterns and dependencies more visible

The LGAM is a work in progress and will continue to evolve as we work alongside local government to refine it.

For more information on how the LGAM was created, read our blogs on:

  • sourcing the stack for local government technology
  • an understanding of technical architecture in local government
  • how we arrived at the first iteration of the model

Layer descriptions

Public channels

The medium through which a service user interacts with the council (e.g., Phone, Online, In-Person).

  • In person: Some council services will still need to be delivered in-person. This includes at a council’s offices, at a household or business, or at another location.
  • Phone: Some services are delivered via telephone, allowing users to speak directly with council staff for support, enquiries, or transactions where digital access may not be suitable/available to the user.
  • Email: Asynchronous written communication via email.
  • Letter: Some services rely on postal communication, particularly for formal notifications, legal correspondence or where physical record of delivery is required.
  • Online: Many council services are delivered online through websites, portals or forms, enabling users to self-serve at a time that suits them.
  • SMS/ Messaging: Some services may use SMS or messaging channels to provide updates, reminders or quick interactions.
  • Social Media: Some councils may use social media platforms to share information, respond to enquiries and engage with residents in a more informal/accessible way.
  • Smart Device: Some services can be accessed through smart devices such as Internet of Things, to enable automated interaction where appropriate.
  • Third Party: Users may sometimes access council services through proxy; by going through their local MP or local media.
  • Video: Some councils may use video calls to deliver service remotely, allowing users to speak face-to-face with staff without needing to attend in person.

Council agents

Agents are interfaces which allow service users to contact and use a council’s services.

  • App: Native or cross-platform mobile application providing dedicated services to citizens.
  • API Gateway: Machine-to-machine interface handling third-party integrations and smart device inputs.
  • Automated Phone: Automated telephony system, such as IVR or conversational voicebots.
  • Human: Customer Service Representatives or specialist officers handling queries.
  • Public Device: These are devices owned by the council that are in the public domain, and include technology such as touch-screens in a public location for example in a library, or an internet of things device for example in a drain or bin.
  • Website: The primary council web portal, forms, and digital content interfaces.

Capabilities

Capabilities are tools that council agents use to meet the needs of their service users.

  • Agentic AI: Autonomous AI agents capable of reasoning, executing multi-step tasks, and making routing decisions.
  • Booking: Councils use booking tools to arrange and manage appointments or service slots, such as inspections, and interviews with users.
  • Email: Emails are used as the primary tool to send and receive messages when dealing with users, internal/external stakeholders.
  • Forms: Forms are used to collect and check information provided by users, such as in applications, reports and requests.
  • Identity: Councils use identity tools to check and verify who a user is.
  • Payments: Payment tools are used to take and track payments made by users for services, such as council tax, licences or permits.
  • SMS: Councils use SMS tools to send and receive text messages for updates, reminders and quick communications with users.
  • Telephony / Fax: Councils use telephone (and in some cases where still needed, fax) to communicate/exchange information with their users.
  • Workflow: These are used to manage and track users request/application/casework as it moves through different processing stages.

Business and corporate areas

The overarching service domains or directorates defined by the Local Government Association (LGA) standard functions.

  • Administration and government: Core governance and administrative operations.
  • Advice and benefits: Welfare rights, financial support, and resident advisory services.
  • Business and employment: Local enterprise support and economic development.
  • Community safety: Emergency planning and public protection services.
  • Environmental protection: Health, conservation, and sustainability.
  • Health and social care: Statutory adult and children's social services and public health.
  • Housing: Social housing management, repairs, and homelessness prevention.
  • Leisure and culture: Management of parks, arts venues, and local heritage.
  • Planning and building control: Spatial planning and construction regulation.
  • Transport and highways: Infrastructure management and transport schemes.
