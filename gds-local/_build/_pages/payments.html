    Payments

    Capabilities used to take and track payments made by users for council services such as council tax, planning application fees, parking permits, and licence fees.

    About this capability

    Payment capabilities sit in the Capabilities layer of the Local Government Architecture Model. They enable council interfaces (websites, apps, automated phone systems) to collect money from citizens and businesses for a wide range of services.

    Most councils use payment platforms that integrate with their existing income management and finance systems. The choice of platform affects citizen experience, staff workflows, PCI compliance burden, and ongoing costs.

    This page provides guidance on sourcing payment capabilities, the platforms available, and relevant standards.

    What to consider

    Key questions when sourcing or evaluating payment capabilities:

    • PCI-DSS compliance: Does the platform handle payment card data securely, keeping it off your servers?
    • Payment types: Do you need one-off payments, recurring payments, or both? Does it support refunds and partial payments?
    • Reconciliation: Can payment data flow automatically into your income management or finance systems?
    • Channel support: Can citizens pay online, by phone (IVR), and in person from a single platform?
    • Accessibility: Does the payment journey meet WCAG 2.2 AA standards and work with assistive technologies?
    • Procurement: Is the platform available without a formal procurement process, or does it require competitive tendering?

    Available platforms

    GDS

    GOV.UK Pay is a free, PCI-DSS compliant payment platform for all public sector organisations. No procurement process is needed. It supports Stripe, Worldpay, and the Government Banking Barclaycard contract as payment service providers.

    You can use it in two ways:

    • Without code: Create a payment link in minutes and add it to an email, letter, or webpage.
    • With code: Integrate the REST API directly into your digital services for a seamless user journey.

    Commercial

    Capita Pay360 — payment processing and income management platform widely used across UK local government. Supports online, telephone, and in-person payments with back-office reconciliation.

    Heycentric — digital payment and customer engagement platform for local government, combining payment processing with citizen self-service.

    Standards and interoperability

    Payment capabilities in local government should align with the following standards:

    • PCI-DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard) — mandatory for any system handling card payment data
    • SCA / 3DS2 (Strong Customer Authentication) — required under UK payment regulations for online card transactions
    • Open Banking APIs — emerging standard for bank-to-bank payments, potentially reducing card processing fees
    • GOV.UK Design System — payment journey patterns for consistent, accessible citizen-facing interfaces
    • WCAG 2.2 AA — accessibility standard that all public sector digital services must meet

    Technical patterns

    Common integration and architecture patterns for payment capabilities in council systems.

    API integration patterns

    Most payment platforms offer RESTful JSON APIs for programmatic integration. Common patterns include redirect flows (where the citizen is sent to a hosted payment page and returned on completion) and embedded flows (where payment fields are rendered within your own service). Webhooks provide real-time notifications of payment events (such as payment_captured or payment_failed) for automated reconciliation with back-office systems.

    Security and compliance

    A PCI-DSS compliant platform keeps card data entirely off your own servers, significantly reducing your compliance burden. Look for platforms that handle Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) and 3D Secure 2 (3DS2) natively, as these are regulatory requirements for online card payments. Support for digital wallets (Apple Pay, Google Pay) can improve completion rates on mobile devices.

    Testing and sandbox environments

    When evaluating platforms, look for sandbox or test environments that allow your developers to build and verify integrations using mock card numbers without processing real transactions. This reduces risk during development and enables continuous integration testing.

    Connected business areas

    Business areas from the LGAM where payment capabilities are typically used.

    • Planning and development — application fees, community infrastructure levy, land charges
    • Revenues and benefits — council tax, business rates, overpayment recovery
    • Housing — rent payments, service charges, right-to-buy deposits
    • Licensing and regulation — licence fees, permit charges, fixed penalty notices
    • Highways and transport — parking permits, penalty charge notices, skip licences
    • Leisure and culture — facility bookings, event tickets, library fees
    • Education — school meals, trip contributions, music tuition
    • Waste management — bulky waste collections, garden waste subscriptions
